## Where I’ve Been (and Why I Disappeared)

Over the last year, I’ve been unemployed, which comes with a challenge that’s easy to underestimate: staying motivated when nothing is forcing you to. No deadlines. No external expectations. Just you, your plans, and however much discipline you can muster on a given day. For a while, I managed. I built a loose routine: working on personal projects, sending out CVs, slowly making progress. It wasn’t perfect, but it worked.

Then about two months ago, it didn’t. The war with Iran hit, and like everyone else here, my daily life got thrown off balance. We were waking up at 3 AM most nights. Sleep was inconsistent. Going outside didn’t always feel safe. Businesses shut down. Any routine I had built just… collapsed.

And once the structure disappeared, something else went with it: my motivation.

## What Actually Broke

Looking back, the surprising part isn’t that I lost motivation. it’s how quickly it happened once my structure was gone. Before the war, I had a system (even if it was a bit messy):

- One main project per week  
- A few hours each day for job applications  
- Enough variety to stay engaged  

It wasn’t high-performance, but it was steady. And it got results. this blog is one of them. But that system depended on something I didn’t fully appreciate at the time: **structure**. Not just a schedule, but a sense that my time had shape and boundaries.

When the war disrupted that, the system fell apart. And once I broke my own commitments, it became harder to restart. That spiral is familiar: less structure → less motivation → less action → even less motivation.

## The Turning Point (Unexpectedly)

Interestingly, what helped me break out of that loop wasn’t doubling down on discipline.

It was accidentally reintroducing structure from the outside.

With less energy for my usual projects, I started exploring alternative income options. That led me back to something I hadn’t focused on in a while: teaching.

I began tutoring Math and English, and joined the Gefen program through Kidum, working with high school students from northern Israel who had also been heavily affected by the war.

And that changed things.

Suddenly:

- I had fixed hours  
- People were relying on me  
- There were real consequences to not showing up  

In other words, I had structure again, and with it, my motivation started to come back.


## What I Took From This

This whole period forced me to think more clearly about something I used to treat vaguely: the relationship between motivation and structure.

Here’s where I’ve landed:

### 1. Motivation isn't reliable - structure is.

When all your work depends on internal drive, it works, until it doesn’t. I was able to stay productive for months on self-imposed discipline. But once something disrupted that (in this case, the war), there was nothing holding it up.

What I had wasn’t real structure. It was a well-maintained illusion of it. Actual structure, at least for me, comes from **external commitments**: things that don’t disappear just because I’m having an off week.

### 2. Projects should survive “crash periods”

One thing I did get right, almost by accident, was how I built my projects. They’re all:

- Modular  
- Well-documented  
- Easy to pause and resume  

So even after weeks of doing nothing, I’m not starting from zero. I can pick things back up without friction. That’s been incredibly valuable, and I’m now thinking of it more deliberately: every project should be able to survive a period where I disappear from it.

### 3. Novelty matters more than I thought

My weekly rotation between projects helped keep things fresh. Every time I came back to something, it felt slightly new again. Teaching ended up doing the same thing on a bigger scale. It was different enough from my usual work to reset my energy. That shift alone made it easier to get moving again.

## Where I Am Now

I’m still figuring things out. I don’t suddenly have perfect discipline, and I’m not pretending I’ve solved motivation. But I understand my own patterns better now.

If there’s one thing this period made clear, it’s this: Relying on discipline alone is a fragile strategy. What works better, for me at least, is designing my life so that structure exists outside of my mood:

- Commitments to other people  
- Work that has consequences  
- Systems that don’t collapse if I miss a few days  

I still want to expand what I’m doing: more projects, more directions to explore. But now I’m thinking about those plans differently, and a fresh perspective is always motivating.
